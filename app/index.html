Design brief

Erik Forsberg

What is this?

Thinking about the word "ambient", what popped up into my mind was the ambiance of a deep ocean. Therefore, what you see is an infinitely looping underwater visualization of VIC:s Facebook likes made with D3.js, TweenMax, jQuery, and a bunch of custom JS along with HTML and CSS.

The bubbles

The bubbles represent every day's new likes. The size of the bubbles vary according to the new likes that day. The bubbles' movement is psuedo-random, as is the duration of their life. They slowly fade during the animation until they pop and disappear.

The sky

As the visualization runs, the sky subtly gets brighter and brighter. The brightness represents the lifetime likes of the page, representing a day when it starts out dark with few likes and ends bright with over 1000 likes in February 2016.

Looking at the sunburst in the second slide of the left half of the sky (when information of toggled), one can see the demography of the likes at the end of the timeline.

Interactivity

The user can control the animation by clicking on the little down-facing caret at the top of the ocean. Below is a short explanation of the controls.

The user can pause and restart the animation, change the playback speed to reach a super ambient slowmotion speed, play it quickly, or even play it backwards. The rightmost slider is a filter, showing only bubbles representing an amount of likes larger than the chosen value. Toggling the information panels, one can over over or click a bubble for information about that day.

Ambiance

The playback speed control is inspired by the second lesson in the study by Skog et al. They say that the rate of change in the information should be frequent enough to promote relevance, however by slowing down the changes and adding a small amount of animation we can make the visualization very calm and ambient. This is achieved by dragging down the animation speed using the slider to a point where the bubbles just barely move at a quick glance, and toggling off the information boxes to not focus the attention to the ticking lifetime likes in the left-hand part of the sky.

By not including a bunch of different colors, keeping to a rather narrow ocean-like palette gives a very calm experience.
